Extreme Heat
Temps above 90°F for at least 2–3 days. Your body works extra hard to stay cool.
ready.gov/heat
🌊
Heat Wave
Prolonged excessive heat, often with humidity. Warm nights don't give our bodies time to cool down.
ready.gov
🚨
Heat Advisory
Heat index values of 100–104°F for 2+ hours. Seek shade and cool spaces!
mass.gov
⚠️
Heat Warning
Dangerous heat is happening NOW. Avoid heavy activity and direct sunlight.
weather.gov
💧
Humidity
Moisture in the air. Stops sweat evaporating, making your body feel even hotter.
ready.gov/heat
🌬️
Heat Index
How hot it actually FEELS when humidity is factored in. Can feel much hotter than the thermometer!
mass.gov
🏥
Heat Stroke
Most serious heat emergency. Body can no longer cool itself — can cause brain damage or death. Call 9-1-1!
mass.gov / ready.gov
🏫
Cooling Center
Free public place — library, museum, mall — to escape the heat. Find one at 211.org.
ready.gov
😵
Dehydration
Body loses too much water. Signs: dizziness, dry mouth, dark urine. Drink before you feel thirsty!
ready.gov/heat
😓
Heat Exhaustion
Dizziness, heavy sweating, nausea, weakness. Move to cool area, loosen clothing, sip cool water.
weather.gov

Chapter 3

How to Stay Cool!

Before, during, and after extreme heat — sourced from Ready.gov and FEMA.

▸ BEFORE a Heat Wave
📋

Make a Plan

Create a family communications plan. Know your nearest cooling center before it gets hot.

🎒

Build a Kit

Have water, food, and supplies for at least 3 days. Get your kids involved!

📡

Stay Informed

Listen to local weather forecasts and sign up for emergency alerts in your community.

▸ DURING Extreme Heat
💧

Drink Water

Even when not thirsty. Avoid alcohol, caffeine, and sugary drinks.

🏠

Stay Indoors

Limit sun exposure. Fans alone may not be enough — find air conditioning.

👒

Dress Smart

Loose, lightweight, light-colored clothes. Dark colors absorb the sun's rays.

🚗

Never Stay in a Car

Temperatures rise quickly even with windows cracked. Never leave a child alone in a vehicle.

🏫

Use Cooling Centers

Go to a library, museum, or mall. Find one at 211.org.

🧡

Check on Others

Older adults and young children are most at risk. Check on them during heat waves.

Source: ready.gov/heat, ready.gov/kids

For Parents 👨‍👩‍👧

All information sourced from Ready.gov, FEMA, and the National Weather Service. Use the tabs below to explore each section.

🔔Practice Safety DrillsReady.gov▾
Practice evacuation plans and emergency procedures with children on a regular basis. Kids who rehearse are far less likely to panic during an actual emergency. Include heat scenarios — where to go, who to call, how to stay cool.
Source: ready.gov/kids
💬Talk About Heat IllnessFEMA▾
Discuss physical symptoms and warning signs of heat-related illnesses so your child can recognize danger in themselves and others. Use age-appropriate language — for young children, keep it simple: "If you feel dizzy, too hot, or confused, tell an adult right away."
Source: ready.gov/heat, fema.gov
📞Teach Kids to Call 9-1-1Critical▾
Teach kids when and how to call 9-1-1. Make sure they have an emergency contact memorized or written down. Create a family password or phrase to prevent your child from going with a stranger during an emergency.
Source: ready.gov/kids
🧠Why Preparation MattersResearch▾
Children who are prepared experience less anxiety and feel more confident during actual emergencies and disasters. Being part of the preparedness process also builds responsibility, teamwork, and real-world problem-solving skills.
"Prepare your family before the emergency occurs." — FEMA
🚨 Call 9-1-1 immediately if you see signs of Heat Stroke

😓 Heat Exhaustion

  • Dizziness or fainting
  • Heavy sweating
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Weakness, fatigue
  • Cool, pale, moist skin
  • Fast, weak pulse
Move to cool area. Loosen clothing. Sip cool water. Seek help if no improvement in 1 hour.

🆘 Heat Stroke

  • Extremely high body temp
  • Hot, red, dry OR moist skin
  • Rapid, strong pulse
  • Confusion or unconsciousness
  • Vomiting or seizures
  • Slurred speech
⚠️ CALL 9-1-1 NOW. Move to cool area, remove outer clothing, cool with ice or cold water.

🧠What happens during heat stroke?▾
Heat stroke develops when systems in the body begin to stop functioning due to extreme heat. It can cause brain damage or death if the body is not cooled quickly. Heat stroke can occur even during moderate heat — not just on the hottest days.
Source: mass.gov, weather.gov
👶Why are children at higher risk?▾
Children's bodies warm at a faster rate than adults. They are less able to regulate their own temperature and less likely to recognize warning signs. The temperature inside a parked vehicle can rapidly rise to dangerous levels — never leave a child in a car, even with windows cracked.
Source: ready.iowa.gov, ready.gov/heat
🐾Don't forget pets!▾
If your pets are outside, make sure they have plenty of cool water and access to comfortable shade. Asphalt and dark pavement can be very hot to your pet's feet. Never leave pets in a vehicle — call 911 if you see a pet or child in a hot car.
Source: ready.gov/heat

Extreme heat doesn't just affect the body — it can affect children's emotions and mental wellbeing too. Here's what to watch for and how to help.

⚠️ Warning Signs to Watch For
😟Mood & Behavior Changes▾
Heat stress can make children more irritable, anxious, or withdrawn than usual. Watch for:
  • Increased irritability or emotional outbursts
  • Unusual quietness or withdrawal from family
  • Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
  • Complaints of feeling overwhelmed or scared
Source: cdc.gov
😴Sleep & Physical Stress Signals▾
Heat disrupts sleep, which worsens emotional regulation in children. Signs include:
  • Trouble falling or staying asleep
  • Nightmares or increased fear at bedtime
  • Unexplained stomachaches or headaches
  • Regression to younger behaviors (thumb-sucking, bedwetting)
Source: cdc.gov
😰Anxiety About Weather & Safety▾
Some children develop worry or fear around extreme heat events, especially after a frightening experience. Look for:
  • Repeated questions about heat danger
  • Refusing to go outside even when it's safe
  • Clinging or separation anxiety during heat warnings
  • Intrusive worries about family members' safety
Source: cdc.gov
💚 Coping Strategies for Families
💬Talk Openly & Calmly▾
Use age-appropriate language to explain what's happening. Validate your child's feelings without amplifying fear. Try saying: "It's okay to feel worried — we have a plan and we're safe." Avoid dismissing concerns or over-explaining worst-case scenarios.
Source: cdc.gov
📅Keep Routines Stable▾
Predictable routines help children feel safe during stressful events. Try to maintain regular mealtimes, bedtimes, and play — even if activities shift indoors. Involve children in simple tasks like filling water bottles to give them a sense of control.
Source: cdc.gov
🎨Stay Active & Creative Indoors▾
Physical movement and creative play reduce anxiety and restlessness during periods spent indoors. Ideas: indoor dance breaks, drawing, building forts, board games, reading, or simple breathing exercises. Physical activity — even brief — helps regulate children's emotions.
Source: cdc.gov
🧘Model Calm Behavior▾
Children take emotional cues from caregivers. Your calm, matter-of-fact response to heat events teaches children that challenges are manageable. Practice deep breathing or mindfulness together — even 2–3 slow breaths can help reset an anxious child.
Source: cdc.gov
